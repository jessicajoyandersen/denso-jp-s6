Block A — The System
This, That, That Over There — and Which?
Japanese organizes "pointing" words by distance — from you, from the listener, or far from both. Once you see the pattern, every word in the system falls into place.
The Big Idea
Japanese has four pointing roots: ko- (close to me), so- (close to you), a- (far from both of us), and do- (question). Every demonstrative word in Japanese is built from one of these four roots. Learn the roots — you get the whole system.

On a manufacturing floor, this unlocks real communication: "Is it this part or that part?" "That machine over there." "Which station?"

💡 Floor Context
The so- / a- distinction matters most at DENSO. If a colleague is standing at their station holding a part: sore (that — near you). If both of you are looking across the floor at something: are (that over there). In practice, pointing + context makes this clear even when you're not sure which to use.
📝 S5 Correction — Responding to Yoroshiku Onegaishimasu
In Session 5 we said a nod is a sufficient response when receiving work with yoroshiku onegaishimasu. That was a Western-lens mistake — in a Japanese workplace context this is actually a reciprocal exchange, and a verbal response is expected. Here are the natural options:
Hai, wakarimashita. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.
はい、わかりました。よろしくお願いします。
Yes, understood. I'll take good care of it. — mirrors the yoroshiku back, closes the loop
Omakase kudasai.
お任せください。
Please leave it to me. — confident, honorific; appropriate when receiving from a senior
⚠️ Not this: Watashi ni makasete kudasai — too casual. The honorific form omakase kudasai is correct when responding to a senior.
⚠️ Not this: Kochira koso yoroshiku onegaishimasu — implies a mutual exchange of work between equals. That's not the dynamic when receiving an assignment.

Block C — Modifier Form
Kono / Sono / Ano / Dono + Noun
Add a noun and you go from pointing at a thing to naming it. "This part." "That machine." "Which station?"
The Rule
Kore/Sore/Are/Dore stand alone — they are the thing. Kono/Sono/Ano/Dono come before a noun — they describe the thing. Drop the final -re, add -no: kore → kono. Same roots, same spatial logic.

Block D — Negation
Ja Nai Desu — "It's not ~"
The flip side of desu. Once you can say what something is, you need to say what it isn't — especially when correcting mistakes on the floor.
Why This Matters First
On a production floor, the most important thing you can do is flag a mistake fast. "It's not this part." "That's not the right procedure." "Not here — over there." These corrections prevent errors from moving down the line.

ja nai desu is the polite, professional negation form. It connects directly to the X wa Y desu pattern from Session 5 — same structure, negated ending.
The Pattern
Positive → Negative
Affirmative
[X] desu.
It is [X].
→
Negative
[X] ja nai desu.
It is not [X].
Replace desu → ja nai desu. Everything else stays the same.
Romaji / PhoneticJapaneseFloor context
Kore ja nai desu.
koh-reh jah nigh des
⚠️ floor-critical
これじゃないです。
 It's not this one. Pointing at the wrong part to flag an error.
Sore ja nai desu.
soh-reh jah nigh des
⚠️ floor-critical
それじゃないです。
 That's not it. The listener is holding or pointing at the wrong item.
Koko ja nai desu.
koh-koh jah nigh des
ここじゃないです。
 Not here. Correcting a location — wrong bin, wrong station, wrong placement.
Kono pātsu ja nai desu.
koh-noh pah-tsoo jah nigh des
このパーツじゃないです。
 It's not this part. More specific than "kore ja nai desu" — names the category.
Yamada-san ja nai desu.
yah-mah-dah-sahn jah nigh des
山田さんじゃないです。
 It's not Yamada-san (it's someone else). Works for any name — clarifying who someone was asking for.
⚠️ Tone — How to say this without sounding harsh
In English, "it's not this one" can land abruptly. In Japanese, ja nai desu is already the polite form — it's softer than it sounds in translation. For extra softness, add sumimasen first: Sumimasen, sore ja nai desu. This is not required, but it's the norm when correcting a colleague.

💬 Responding to Yoroshiku Onegaishimasu
When a colleague — especially a senior — passes work to you with yoroshiku onegaishimasu, this is a reciprocal exchange. A nod alone is below the expected register. Here are the natural responses:
Hai, wakarimashita. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.
はい、わかりました。よろしくお願いします。
Yes, understood. I'll take good care of it. — mirrors the yoroshiku back, closes the loop
Omakase kudasai.
お任せください。
Please leave it to me. — confident, honorific; appropriate when receiving from a senior
⚠️ Not this one: Kochira koso yoroshiku onegaishimasu implies a mutual exchange — like both parties are entrusting work to each other. That's not the case when receiving an assignment, so it doesn't fit here.
New colleague asks which machine to use.
Colleague
Dono kikai desu ka?
どの機械ですか？
Which machine is it?
You
Ano kikai desu.
あの機械です。
It's that machine over there. (pointing across the floor)
