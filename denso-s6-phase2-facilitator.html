💬 Talking Points
›
Open with the frame: "Japanese demonstratives aren't random — they're a system. Learn the four roots and you get all twelve words for free."
›
Write on the board or point to the grid: ko = near me, so = near you, a = far from both, do = question. Say these out loud and have the group repeat.
›
Key insight to verbalize: "The suffix tells you the category. -re = identify an object. -ko = identify a location. -no = attach to a noun. Every row follows this pattern."
›
Floor anchor: "Think about pointing at a part on your line. If it's right in front of you — ko. If it's at the person you're talking to — so. If it's across the room — a."
✎
S5 Correction — flag this before moving on. In Session 5 we said a nod is sufficient when receiving yoroshiku onegaishimasu. That was a Western-lens mistake. This is a reciprocal exchange — a verbal response is expected. Walk through the two correct options on the learner screen: Hai, wakarimashita. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu. and Omakase kudasai. Also clarify what not to say: Watashi ni makasete kudasai is too casual; Kochira koso yoroshiku onegaishimasu implies a mutual work exchange, which doesn't fit.
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Don't get into -no vs. -re yet — that's Block C. This block is about the four spatial roots only. If someone asks "what about kono?" say "that's next — same root, different ending."
💡
The grid can be overwhelming at first glance. Reassure them: "You don't memorize all twelve. You learn the four roots, and the rest falls out of the pattern."
✎
S5 correction: If learners already know watashi ni makasete kudasai from a previous source, gently redirect — the honorific omakase kudasai is the right form when responding to a senior assigning work. Don't let the casual form get reinforced.

Block A.5
Interactive Spatial Map + Sentence Builders
💬 Talking Points
›
Direct learners to the spatial map: "Click a zone — the screen will show you which root applies and why. Pay attention to where YOU are and where the OTHER PERSON is."
›
Walk through each zone together: click ko-, so-, a-, do- and read the explanation aloud. The do- label floats at the bottom — remind them it's not a place, it's a question.
›
Key question to ask the group: "If I'm holding a bolt and you want to tell me it's the wrong one — which root do you use?" (Answer: so-, because the bolt is near the listener.)
›
Quick check: point to something across the room. "Which root?" (a-). Point to something at a learner's desk. "Which root?" (so-). Point to something you're holding. "Which root?" (ko-).
👁 Watch For
💡
Learners often default to ko- for everything. The spatial map click interaction is designed to break that habit — make sure they actually click so- and a- zones, not just ko-.
⚠️
Keep this block short — the map is a bridge from Block A concepts to Block B vocabulary. Don't let discussion here eat into Block B or C time.
👉 Block B — Standalone Forms
Block B · 6–7 min · kore/sore/are/dore + koko/soko/asoko/doko
3 / 6
Block B
Standalone Forms — kore / sore / are / dore
These stand alone — no noun attached. Point at a thing or a location and they do the whole job.
⏱
Suggested time
6–7 min
Physical pointing makes this click — do it
💬 Talking Points
›
Stand up and physically demonstrate. Point at something near you: kore desu. Point at something near a learner: sore desu. Point across the room: are desu. Then ask "dore desu ka?" sweeping your hand across all three.
›
Repeat for locations: stamp where you're standing (koko), point toward a learner's desk (soko), point at the far wall or door (asoko), then spread your hands: doko desu ka?
›
Quick drill: point to random things in the room and have learners call out the right form. Keep it fast — this should feel like a game, not a test.
›
Useful floor phrases to walk through: kore wa nan desu ka? (what's this?), doko desu ka? (where is it?), dore desu ka? (which one?)
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Most common error: using kore when the object is near the listener. Drill this specifically — point at something on a learner's desk and ask "kore or sore?" The answer is sore, because it's near them.
💡
The location series often surprises people — they expect koko/soko/asoko to follow from kore/sore/are, but asoko breaks the -re pattern. Point this out: "asoko is the irregular one. Everything else is clean."
🔤 Block C — Modifier Forms
Block C · 5–6 min · kono/sono/ano/dono + noun
4 / 6
Block C
Modifier Forms — kono / sono / ano / dono + noun
Drop -re, add -no, attach to a noun. This form is more specific than the standalone — you name exactly what you're pointing at.
⏱
Suggested time
5–6 min
The rule is simple — give them floor nouns to practice with
💬 Talking Points
›
Give them the rule explicitly: "Drop the -re, add -no, then say the noun. Kore → kono. Sore → sono. Are → ano. Dore → dono." Have them say the transformation aloud.
›
Practice with floor nouns from their vocabulary reference: kono paatsu (this part), sono boruto (that bolt near you), ano kikai (that machine over there), dono rain? (which line?)
›
Point to things in the room and have them use the modifier form. "You can't just say kono — you need kono something. That's what makes it a modifier."
›
Contrast with standalone: "Kore = that thing. Kono + noun = that specific thing. On a parts-heavy floor, the noun form is often more useful."
›
At the end of Block C, there's a Sentence Builder on the learner screen. Learners need to tap ▶ Open Builder first to reveal it. Give them 2 minutes to try it — pick a root, pick a suffix, pick a noun. Then ask a pair to read their sentence aloud. Quick and low-stakes before moving to negation.
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Kore + noun is wrong. Learners sometimes say "kore paatsu" instead of "kono paatsu." Be clear: kore is standalone only. The modifier form always ends in -no.
💡
If learners ask about "ano" sounding different from the friendly filler word "ano…" (um…), they're right — it's the same syllable but different meaning. Tone and context distinguish them. Don't dwell on it.
🚫 Block D — Ja Nai Desu
Block D · 7–8 min · negation + back-chain drill
5 / 6
Block D
Ja Nai Desu — It's Not This One
The negation pattern that makes every demonstrative correctable. Short, floor-critical, and pair-able with sumimasen for instant politeness.
⏱
Suggested time
7–8 min
Heaviest content in Phase 2 — budget the back-chain time, then let the builder run
💬 Talking Points
›
Show the transformation on the board: kore desu → kore ja nai desu. Just insert "ja nai" before desu. Same thing with sore, are, kore, and location words.
›
Floor application: "Imagine your colleague grabs the wrong bolt. You don't want to say dame (too blunt for a parts mix-up). You say: sumimasen, sore ja nai desu. Polite, clear, done."
›
Run the back-chain drill together — it's in their learner view: nai desu → ja nai desu → kore ja nai desu → sumimasen, kore ja nai desu. Build it up step by step, group repeating each.
›
The full correction pattern is two parts: sumimasen, X ja nai desu + Y desu. Not X — it's Y. Have them practice this with a partner for 60 seconds.
›
After the drill, there's a sentence builder at the bottom of Block D. Learners need to tap ▶ Open Builder first to reveal it. It has three steps: pick a topic (Yamada-san, Tanaka-san, watashi, kore, sore, etc.), pick what it isn't (a job role or A/B-line), pick what it is. The screen assembles the full sentence and gives a verdict — natural, grammatically okay, or doesn't work with an explanation. Give them 2 minutes to explore, then ask someone to read their sentence aloud. The 🔊 button only appears on green (natural) sentences.
👁 Watch For
⚠️
Tone warning is real. Without sumimasen, "sore ja nai desu" can land as blunt — especially across a language gap. Make sure learners add sumimasen and a slight nod. Model it yourself with and without the softener so they hear the difference.
⚠️
Don't introduce ja arimasen (the more formal negation) here. Ja nai desu is the right register for floor communication. Adding formality options now will muddy the message.
💡
The builder flags category mismatches — e.g. "kore wa enjinia ja nai desu" gets an ⚠️ with an explanation that a thing can't have a job title. These are teachable moments. If a learner hits one, pause and discuss why before moving on.
💡
The 🔊 button only appears when the sentence is green. If learners ask why they can't hear a sentence, that's the point — it's not floor-ready.

Block E
Floor Exchanges — Everything Together
Full 2–3 turn exchanges combining new demonstratives + Session 5 phrases. Read aloud, then discuss. Closes Phase 2.
⏱
Suggested time
5–6 min
Keep it light — this bridges into Phase 3
💬 Talking Points
›
Read through the exchanges on their screen together. Have pairs say the lines out loud — not just read. The physical act of saying the exchange matters.
›
Point out where S5 phrases appear naturally: "See how otsukaresama shows up at the close? And yoroshiku in the handoff? That's real layering — new content on a familiar frame."
›
After the exchanges, ask: "Which of these four exchanges would you most likely use this week?" Let two or three people answer. It cements the relevance before Phase 3.
›
Close Phase 2: "Phase 3 starts with cue cards — same idea as today but with a partner. Then 10 scenarios. You've got the vocabulary. Now you practice reading the situation."
👁 Watch For
💡
Some pairs will rush through the exchanges silently. Encourage them to speak aloud — even a quiet voice counts. The muscle memory of saying the phrase is the point.
💡
If time is short (Phase 2 running long), it's fine to skip the exchanges and just review the quick reference grid. The grid is in their learner view and they can use it in Phase 3.
